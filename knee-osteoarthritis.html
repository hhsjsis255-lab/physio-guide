Physio Study Guide

Knee Osteoarthritis Assessment

Patient Profile

Age (usually elderly), occupation, activity level, obesity

Subjective Assessment

  • Knee pain (gradual onset)
  • Morning stiffness
  • Pain during walking or stairs
  • Crepitus (grating sensation)
  • Aggravating factors (activity)
  • Relieving factors (rest)

Objective Assessment

  • Observation (varus/valgus deformity)
  • Swelling
  • Palpation (joint line tenderness)
  • Range of Motion
  • Muscle strength (quadriceps weakness)
  • Gait analysis

Step-wise Clinical Assessment

  1. Introduction & consent
  2. History taking
  3. Check red flags
  4. Observation (alignment)
  5. Palpation
  6. ROM assessment
  7. Muscle strength testing
  8. Functional assessment (walking, stairs)
  9. Clinical reasoning
  10. Physiotherapy diagnosis

🧠 Clinical Reasoning

Pain during activity → degenerative changes
Morning stiffness → osteoarthritis
Crepitus → cartilage degeneration

Clinical Decision Flow

Knee pain → Check age?
↓
Older age → OA likely
Younger → injury?
↓
Pain on movement → degenerative
Swelling → inflammation

Differential Diagnosis

  • Osteoarthritis
  • Ligament injury
  • Meniscus injury

Red Flags 🚨

  • Severe swelling
  • Infection signs
  • Sudden inability to walk

Viva Questions

  • What are features of knee osteoarthritis?
  • What is crepitus?
  • Why is morning stiffness important?
  • How do you differentiate OA from ligament injury?

Key Points for Exam 📌

  • OA is degenerative condition
  • Pain increases with activity
  • Crepitus is a key sign
  • Quadriceps weakness is common

Common Mistakes ⚠️

  • Not assessing gait
  • Ignoring joint alignment (varus/valgus)
  • Skipping functional assessment
  • Confusing OA with ligament injury
