Physio Study Guide

Cervical Spondylosis Assessment

Patient Profile

Age, occupation (desk job/mobile use), lifestyle

Subjective Assessment

  • Neck pain and stiffness
  • Radiating pain to arm
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Headache
  • Aggravating factors (prolonged sitting)
  • Relieving factors

Objective Assessment

  • Observation (forward head posture)
  • Palpation (muscle tightness)
  • Cervical ROM
  • Muscle strength
  • Neurological exam
  • Special tests (Spurling’s test)

Step-wise Clinical Assessment

  1. Introduction & consent
  2. History taking
  3. Check red flags
  4. Observe posture
  5. Palpation
  6. ROM assessment
  7. Neurological exam
  8. Special tests
  9. Clinical reasoning
  10. Physiotherapy diagnosis

🧠 Clinical Reasoning

Radiating pain → nerve root compression
Local neck pain → muscle strain
Pain on extension → facet involvement

Clinical Decision Flow

Patient presents with pain → Check trauma?
↓
Yes → Rule out fracture 🚨
No → Check radiation
↓
Radiating pain → Nerve involvement
Local pain → Muscle involvement
↓
Check movement restriction
↓
Flexion pain → Disc issue
Extension pain → Facet issue

Differential Diagnosis

  • Cervical disc prolapse
  • Muscle strain
  • Nerve compression

Red Flags 🚨

  • Severe trauma
  • Progressive neurological deficit
  • Unexplained weight loss

Viva Questions

  • What are common symptoms of cervical spondylosis?
  • What is Spurling’s test?
  • What are signs of nerve root compression?
  • Difference between cervical disc and muscle pain?

Key Points for Exam 📌

  • Check posture (forward head posture)
  • Radiating pain indicates nerve involvement
  • Spurling’s test is important
  • Always assess neurological signs

Common Mistakes ⚠️

  • Ignoring posture assessment
  • Not checking neurological symptoms
  • Skipping special tests
  • Confusing muscle pain with nerve pain
